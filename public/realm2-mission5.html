1. The Multi-Layered Traditional Financial System

The modern financial system consists of multiple interconnected layers, each with specific roles and responsibilities. Understanding this structure helps grasp why the system has evolved to be both complex and centralized.

üèõÔ∏è

Central Banks

At the foundation of the financial system are central banks, which serve as the "banks for banks" and act as the monetary authority for countries or regions.

Key responsibilities include:

  1. Monetary Policy: Setting interest rates and managing money supply to achieve economic goals like price stability, full employment, and economic growth.
  2. Currency Issuance: They are responsible for printing physical currency and issuing base money (reserves) to the banking system, which forms the foundation of the broader money supply.
  3. Financial Stability: Central banks act as "lenders of last resort" during financial crises, providing liquidity to prevent bank runs and systemic collapse.
  4. Banking Supervision: Many central banks regulate and supervise the banking system to ensure compliance with laws and maintain stability.
üè¢

Commercial Banks

Commercial banks sit between central banks and the general public/businesses. They provide financial services and products and play a crucial role in money creation through fractional reserve banking.

Key functions include:

  1. Deposit Taking: Accepting deposits from customers and providing security for these funds.
  2. Credit Creation: Creating new money through lending. When a bank issues a loan, it creates a deposit in the borrower's account, effectively creating new money.
  3. Payment Processing: Facilitating payments between individuals, businesses, and organizations.
  4. Investment Services: Offering various investment products and services to customers.
Did you know? Most of the money in modern economies exists not as physical cash but as bank deposits. In the US, physical currency represents less than 10% of the total money supply.

Payment Networks

Payment networks like Visa, Mastercard, and SWIFT form another layer of the financial system, connecting banks and facilitating the movement of money across institutions and borders.

Key characteristics:

  1. Centralized Infrastructure: These networks operate centralized infrastructure that processes and routes transactions between financial institutions.
  2. Fee Structure: They charge fees for their services, adding costs to the movement of money.
  3. Regulatory Compliance: They enforce various compliance requirements, including KYC (Know Your Customer) and AML (Anti-Money Laundering) rules.
  4. Settlement Delays: Despite appearing instant to users, many payments take days to fully settle between institutions.

Financial Service Providers

On top of banks and payment networks are various financial service providers that offer specialized services to customers:

  • Investment firms that manage assets and provide investment advice
  • Insurance companies that offer protection against various risks
  • Brokerages that facilitate security trading
  • Financial technology (fintech) companies that provide innovative financial services

These entities add functionality to the financial system but also introduce additional complexity, costs, and potential points of failure.

2. Challenges of the Traditional Financial Architecture

Settlement Times and Friction

Despite technological advances, many financial transactions still take days to settle:

  • ACH transfers typically take 1-3 business days
  • International wire transfers can take 3-5 business days
  • Check processing can take 2-5 business days
  • Credit card transactions appear instant but actually settle in 1-3 days

These delays occur because transactions must be validated, cleared, and settled through multiple intermediaries, each with their own ledgers and processes.

üí∞

Costs and Fees

The multi-layered structure of the financial system introduces various fees:

  • Account maintenance fees
  • Transaction fees
  • Currency conversion fees (often 2-3% or more)
  • International transfer fees (often $25-50 per transaction)
  • Overdraft fees
  • ATM fees

These fees disproportionately affect lower-income individuals and create significant friction in the movement of money, especially across borders.

üîí

Access and Inclusion Barriers

The traditional system creates significant barriers to financial inclusion:

  • Approximately 1.7 billion adults globally remain unbanked
  • Opening accounts often requires formal identification, proof of address, and minimum deposits
  • Banking services are concentrated in profitable areas, leaving many communities underserved
  • Credit scoring systems can perpetuate historical inequities

These barriers keep many people outside the formal financial system, limiting their economic opportunities and ability to build wealth.

Privacy and Surveillance

Traditional financial institutions collect and store vast amounts of personal and transaction data:

  • Banks know your income, spending habits, location data, and personal details
  • Payment networks track purchasing patterns across merchants
  • Financial data is increasingly shared with governments and third parties
  • Data breaches at financial institutions expose sensitive personal information

This data collection creates a comprehensive financial surveillance system that erodes privacy and can be used for social control and discrimination.

3. Bitcoin: A Different Architectural Approach

Bitcoin represents a fundamental redesign of financial architecture, collapsing multiple layers into a single, integrated system with different properties and characteristics.

‚õìÔ∏è

The Blockchain: A Single Source of Truth

Unlike the traditional system with thousands of separate ledgers that must be reconciled, Bitcoin maintains a single, shared ledger (the blockchain) that:

  • Records all transactions in a transparent, immutable way
  • Is maintained and verified by a distributed network of nodes
  • Eliminates the need for reconciliation between separate ledgers
  • Provides final settlement of transactions within hours rather than days

This single ledger approach removes many intermediaries and simplifies the financial architecture.

üìù

Programmable Money: Self-Executing Rules

Bitcoin integrates monetary policy and rules directly into its code:

  • A predetermined, predictable issuance schedule (approximately 21 million coins total)
  • Transparent rules for transaction validation and processing
  • Absence of discretionary policy decisions that can be influenced by politics
  • Cryptographic assurances rather than legal promises

This approach removes the need for trusted authorities to manage the monetary system and provides certainty about how the system will behave.

üîë

Self-Custody: Direct Asset Control

Bitcoin allows users to directly control their assets without intermediaries:

  • Private keys provide direct access to and control over funds
  • No need to trust third parties to honor withdrawal requests
  • Elimination of counterparty risk associated with custodians
  • Ability to transfer value across borders without permission

This direct control model contrasts sharply with the traditional system where your "money in the bank" is actually a promise from the bank to pay you (an IOU), not actual money you control.

Bitcoin: A Fundamental Redesign

Bitcoin represents not just an incremental improvement to the financial system, but a fundamental redesign. It collapses the multi-layered architecture of traditional finance into a single, integrated system where:

  • The monetary policy is predetermined and executed by code rather than committees
  • The payment system and settlement layer are one and the same
  • Users can directly custody and transfer their assets without intermediaries
  • Transactions are permanently settled within hours rather than days

This elegant simplicity eliminates many points of failure, reduces costs, and expands access to financial services for people around the world, regardless of their socioeconomic status or location.
